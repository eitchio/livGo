The Beatles

Liverpool is where it all started for The Fab Four and there is lots to be seen for any Beatles fan, so keep your camera close. You can visit Penny Lane and walk down to the Dovedale Towers where John Lennon and the Quarrymen performed - as they were initially known (and as a bonus Freddie Mercury rented a room). Take a picture with the larger-than-life statues in the waterfront, or the slightly smaller Eleanor Rigby. Visit the Cavern Club where the boys first performed and take a tour to John and Paul's childhood homes.

Then there is the must-see Beatles Story located on the Albert Dock, the world's largest permanent exhibition dedicated to The Beatles.

Penny Lane

Take a stroll along this famous street and imagine yourself bumping into The Fab Four, many years ago before they were famous. Before you reach the barber on the corner, you will sing along: "Penny Lane is in my ears and in my eyes..."

The Beatles Story

Immerse yourself in the lives of The Beatles and experience how they changed music and culture. This is guaranteed to be a highlight of your Liverpool experience.

The Cavern Club

The venue where The Quarrymen (as they were initially known) first performed on 21 February 1957. Ringo joined the band on 16 August 1962.

Football

Few cities have the privilege of being home to two Premier League teams. Liverpool is firmly divided along Red and Blue lines. Both clubs have stores in the Liverpool One shopping area, where you can stock up on official merchandise.

In the unlikely event that the football is neither here nor there for you, visit the beautiful Stanley Park, from where you can see both stadiums. Most people are surprised at how close the two clubs are to each other.

Goodison Park

Goodison Park, the home of Everton Football Club, was completed in 1892. Before that the club played at...Anfield! (But you probably knew that already!)

Liverpool One/Everton Two

Be sure to visit the official stores in the Liverpool One shopping area, where you can buy kit for the Red or Blue fan in your life. Given the location, Everton's store is called "Everton Two".

Anfield

Anfield has been the home of Liverpool Football Club since their formation in 1892. It was originally the home of Everton F.C. from 1884. A dispute with the club's president caused their move to Goodison Park.

Museums and Galleries

Liverpool is more than 800 years old. The city's fascinating history is captured in the Museum of Liverpool, the UK's first museum dedicated to the history of a city. The beatiful Bluecoat is a Grade I listed building and is considered to be the oldest building in Liverpool. It has a programme across all the creative disciplines. The historic Albert Dock is home to the Tate Liverpool, the International Slavery Museum and the Merseyside Maritime Museum.

The World Museum

This must rate as one of the best places to take your children - and it's free! Between the acquarium, planetarium, ancient Egyptians and dinosaurs, young minds will be entertained for hours.

U-Boat Story

View life on board a real German U-Boat. This is on the Wirral peninsula, opposite Liverpool. Entrance is free if you take the ferry and buy a River Explorer Cruise ticket.

Wirral Transport Museum

This museum is a hidden gem, where you can ride on a real old tram. The museum is currently being modernised and promises to be even better in future.

Architecture

Liverpool has the most historic listed buildings outside of London. A short stroll through the city will make you realise that it is almost unfair to highlight any specific building. The most well-known are "The Three Graces", the Liver Building, The Port of Liverpool Building and the Cunard Building. You can get a terrific view of them on the ferry across the River Mersey to the Wirral peninsula. Then there are the two cathedrals, both in Hope Street, the Town Hall, St. Luke's (better known as the Bombed Out Church). Liverpool is truly a paradise for lovers of architecture.

The Metropolitan Cathedral

The Metropolitan Cathedral of Christ the King has been described as "unapologetically modern". It makes for fascinating comparison with the Gothic style of the Anglican Cathedral down the road.

The Liver Building

The Liver Building (pronounced lie-ver) has the two iconic liver bird statues on top. Legend has it that if they were ever to fly away, Liverpool would cease to exist.

The Bombed Out Church

St. Luke's, or "The Bombed Out Church" as it is more commonly known as, is the site of a World War II bombing. Today it remains as a roofless shell and is well worth a visit. Afterwards you can grab something to eat at one of the many restaurants in the popular Bold Street, opposite the church.

Parks and Gardens

Liverpool has an abundance of beautiful parks. Birkenhead Park on the Wirral is widely regarded as the model for Central Park in New York and the first publicly funded civic park in the world. Calderstones Park is home to the Allerton Oak, a tree that is over 1 000 years old. The local court used to convene under its branches.

A stroll or bicycle-ride down the Otterspool Promenade is well worth your time. Start at the Albert Dock and take in the pleasant views of the Mersey Estuary.

The Palmhouse at Sefton Park

Sefton Park was created in its current form in 1872. The Grade II listed Palmhouse was constructed in 1892. It has eight sculptures of explorers and botanists around it. The park is 235 acres and great for exploring on a bicycle.

Speke Hall

Speke Hall is a Tudor manor house on which construction started in 1530. The gardens have acres of woodlands and a hedge maze to explore.

Ness Botanic Gardens

The gardens are described as a living library. It was established in 1892 by Arthur Kilpin Bulley, who introduced hundreds of Himalayan and Chinese mountain plants to Britain.
